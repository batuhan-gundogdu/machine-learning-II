Lecture-2: Error Surface in Linear Classification

Visualizing the cross-entropy loss landscape in weight space — an interactive guide.

1

Introduction

In linear classification we model the posterior probability of class 1 as

$$y(\mathbf{x};\,\mathbf{w}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{1+\exp(-\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x})}$$

where $\mathbf{w} = (w_1, w_2)$ and the bias is fixed at zero. This tutorial lets you explore how the cross-entropy error surface in $\mathbf{w}$-space relates to the decision boundary in $\mathbf{x}$-space.

2

Setup & Model

We generate two classes from Gaussian distributions that share a covariance matrix $\Sigma$ but have different means $\boldsymbol{\mu}_0$ and $\boldsymbol{\mu}_1$. The cross-entropy (negative log-likelihood) for $N$ data points is:

$$E(\mathbf{w}) = -\sum_{n=1}^{N}\bigl[\,r_n \ln y_n + (1-r_n)\ln(1-y_n)\,\bigr]$$

where $r_n \in \{0,1\}$ is the class label and $y_n = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n)$. With zero bias the decision boundary always passes through the origin: $w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 = 0$.

Interpretation

Darker regions in $\mathbf{w}$-space correspond to lower cross-entropy error — the weight vectors there separate the two classes well. The contour lines (equi-error curves) show sets of weight vectors that achieve the same loss.

When you hover over the error surface, the corresponding decision boundary $w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 = 0$ appears on the $\mathbf{x}$-space plot. Notice how weight vectors in the low-error valley produce boundaries that separate the classes, while those on the high-error plateau produce boundaries that merge or misclassify them.

Because the bias is zero, the boundary is always a line through the origin. The direction of $\mathbf{w}$ determines the orientation of the boundary (the boundary is perpendicular to $\mathbf{w}$), and the magnitude $\|\mathbf{w}\|$ controls the steepness of the sigmoid transition.
